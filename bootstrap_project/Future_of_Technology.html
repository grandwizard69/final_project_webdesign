    Quantum Computing


    Quantum computing
    Quantum computing has been around for a decent bit of time but has been popping up much more recently because of researchers making multiple breakthroughs and developments that could reshape how we see the future.

    Quantum computing works by utilizing qubits, which can exist in multiple states simultaneously and be linked through entanglement, unlike classical bits that are strictly 0 or 1. These properties make it so that quantum computers can process more possibilities in parallel and solve certain problems, such as factoring large numbers (Shor's algorithm) and searching datasets (Grover's algorithm), much faster than classical methods.

    However, despite their potential, quantum computers are in early development, facing challenges like environmental sensitivity, requiring extreme cooling, the need for quantum error correction, and scalability issues to reach millions of qubits for full potential.

    A demonstration of quantum supremacy was achieved by Google in 2019, solving a task much faster than classical supercomputers, though they are not yet ready for everyday use. While currently expensive and accessible primarily to institutions, cloud platforms are increasing remote access for wider experimentation, paving the way for future revolutions in medicine, materials science, AI, and climate modeling.

    Key Aspects and Definitions:
    • Qubits: A qubit is similar to a regular bit(which can either be a 1 or a 0) however a qubit can be both a 1 and 0 thus allowing qubits to hold more information and perform computations in a more parallel way
    • Superposition: A qubit can exist in multiple states (0 and 1) simultaneously until measured, enabling quantum computers to explore many possibilities concurrently.
    • Entanglement: The states of multiple qubits can be linked, meaning they are correlated even if physically separated. This correlated behavior can be exploited to perform computations more efficiently.
    • Quantum algorithms: Quantum computers use specialized algorithms, like Shor's algorithm for factoring large numbers, that can potentially solve problems faster than classical algorithms.
    • Scalability: The scalability of quantum computers will be one of the challenges that we will face.
    • Quantum error correction: A set of techniques and algorithms made to identify and fix errors in quantum computers while also protecting the information stored in qubits from errors and decoherence.
    • Decoherence:The process by which a quantum system loses its unique quantum properties, such as superposition and entanglement, by interacting with its environment

    Majorana 1 chip <------ click to expand!
    The Majorana 1 is the world's first quantum processor based on topological qubits, a fundamentally new kind of qubit protected by the principles of topology rather than the more frail quantum states. It’s built using a topoconductor which is a hybrid material made from indium arsenide (semiconductor) and aluminum (superconductor) that achieves a new topological superconducting state.

    This topological state of matter doesn't behave like ordinary solids, liquids, or gases. It enables Majorana zero modes(or MZM), exotic quasiparticles theorized decades ago, that can encode quantum information in a way highly resistant to noise making it much less frail. In this setup, the information isn’t stored in a fragile superposition but rather in the presence or absence of an extra electron shared between two Majorana zero modes, making the data almost immune to disturbances.

    How it works:
    • Qubit Structure: Each qubit is formed by configuring aluminum nanowires into an “H” shape, with four MZMs controlling one qubit. These H-shaped units can be tiled across the chip for scalability.
    • Measurement-Based Control: Rather than relying on precisely shaped analog pulses like many quantum systems, Majorana-1 uses digital control. You merely flip a switch to connect a quantum dot to the nanowire, and send simple measurement pulses meaning timing and shape don’t need to be perfect.
    • Reading the Qubit: When the quantum dot connects to the nanowire, the change in its electrical charge depends on the qubit’s parity. This change alters how microwaves reflect off the dot, letting us infer the qubit’s state.

    What's Next and What's Uncertain:
    • Roadmap Toward Fault Tolerance: Microsoft envisions progressing from single qubits to multi qubit arrays capable of quantum error correction, ultimately leading to a fault tolerant quantum computer.
    • Skepticism Remains: While Microsoft’s claims are exciting, some in the scientific community are cautious. Independent validation of MZMs and measurable quantum advantage is still pending.

    What is Virtual Reality?

    Virtual Reality (VR) is a technology that immerses users in a virtual environment that can simulate real-world or entirely fictional experiences. Unlike traditional screens, VR surrounds your field of vision, making it feel as though you are “inside” the digital world.

    Headsets achieve this by using high-resolution displays, lenses, sensors, and motion tracking to respond to your movements. Turn your head left or right, and the view in VR shifts accordingly. This creates the illusion of presence, the feeling of actually being in a virtual space.

    How is Virtual Reality used Today?

      VR has expanded beyond gaming into many fields, including:

    • Gaming and Entertainment: Fully immersive gameplay, cinematic experiences, and social VR hangouts.
    • Education and Training: Simulated classrooms, medical training, flight simulators, and job skill development.
    • Healthcare: Therapy for phobias, PTSD treatment, physical rehabilitation, and pain distraction.
    • Architecture and Design: Virtual walkthroughs of buildings and products before they’re built.
    • Social Interaction: Meeting friends in virtual spaces, attending concerts, or working in VR offices.

    Key Features That Define VR Quality:

    • Field of View (FOV): A Wider FOV makes experiences more natural and less like “looking through binoculars.”
    • Resolution and Pixels Per Degree (PPD): Higher resolution reduces the “screen door effect,” where individual pixels are visible.
    • Brightness and Contrast: Important for clear visuals, especially when blending VR with real-world views (mixed reality).
    • Refresh Rate and Latency: Higher refresh rates can reduce motion sickness and make movements appear more fluid.
    • Comfort and Form Factor: A lighter, well-balanced headset makes VR more enjoyable for longer sessions.

    Meta's Role in Virtual Reality

      Meta (formerly Facebook) has become a leader in VR hardware and research. Its consumer line, Meta Quest, has introduced millions of people to virtual reality. But beyond consumer headsets, Meta is also working on experimental prototypes that explore the future of VR visuals and comfort.

      The Meta Quest 3 is currently the flagship consumer headset, offering:

    • Higher resolution than the Quest 2
    • Improved mixed reality passthrough with full-color cameras
    • Slimmer design with “pancake lenses” for comfort


      • The Quest 3S, a more affordable version, keeps many of these features while offering a lower-priced entry into VR.

    Boba 3: Expanding Human Vision in VR

    • Field of View: Approximately 180° horizontal × 120° vertical (very close to the natural human field of vision).
    • Resolution: 4K × 4K per eye.
    • Pixels per Degree (PPD): Around 30, making visuals much clearer.

    • Goal: Maximize immersion by removing the feeling of looking through goggles.
    • Challenge: This headset demands huge amounts of power, is heavy, and is not yet practical for everyday use.

    Tiramisu: Ultra Realistic Visuals For Max Immersion

    • Contrast: Approximately 3 times higher than Quest 3, for deep blacks and vivid highlights.
    • Brightness: Up to ~1,400 nits, which helps in mixed reality environments.
    • Angular Resolution: ~90 PPD (3–4× Quest 3), producing extremely crisp detail.

    • Goal: Reach “hyper realistic” VR visuals that could pass the “visual Turing test.”
    • Challenge: Narrower field of view, bulkier design, and high production costs make it unsuitable for consumers for now.

    What These Prototypes Could Mean for the Future

    • Immersion Will Increase: A Wider FOV will make VR worlds feel almost indistinguishable from reality.
    • Visual Fidelity Will Skyrocket: Prototypes like Tiramisu show how VR could one day match human eye clarity.
    • Headsets Will Specialize: Some may focus on realism (like Tiramisu) while others focus on immersion and comfort (like Boba 3).
    • Mixed Reality Will Evolve: Passthrough tech will improve, blending digital and physical worlds seamlessly.

    • Barriers Remain: High costs, power consumption, heat, and weight need to be solved before these features reach consumers

    Summary

    Virtual reality is rapidly advancing, from today’s affordable consumer devices like the Meta Quest 3 to experimental prototypes that push the boundaries of human perception.

    While headsets like Boba 3 and Tiramisu are not yet ready for the public, they highlight the direction VR is heading: wider fields of view, sharper detail, brighter and more realistic visuals, and eventually, a seamless blend between the real and digital worlds.

    VR is not just for games, as it’s becoming a platform for work, creativity, education, and human connection. As technology improves, VR may evolve into an everyday tool as common as laptops or smartphones.
