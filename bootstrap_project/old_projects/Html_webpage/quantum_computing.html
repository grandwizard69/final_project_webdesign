How Does it Work?

Quantum computing works by incorporating the principles of quantum mechanics and utilizing quantum bits (better known as qubits). Qubits work by being able to be both a one and a zero. An example is if you had a maze(like shown below), a regular bit would have to go through each possible path one by one to find the best one. However, a qubit can see multiple paths at the same time, making it significantly more powerful and faster.

Left(or top) is the regular bits with each color representing a different bit, and the right(or bottom) is a qubit.

You could also view it like flipping a coin. A normal bit is like a coin lying flat, either heads (0) or tails (1). But a qubit is like a coin spinning in the air; it’s sort of both at once until you look at it. This strange property lets quantum computers process way more possibilities in parallel compared to a regular computer.

Another thing to note is entanglement. If two qubits are entangled, changing one will instantly affect the other, even if they’re far apart. Entanglement allows quantum computers to link qubits together in powerful ways, helping them solve certain problems much faster.


Now you may(or may not) be thinking, how do quantum computers take advantage of these features? Quantum algorithms is how. Quantum algorithms are special instructions written to take advantage of superposition and entanglement.

For example, Shor’s algorithm can factor huge numbers much better and more efficiently than classical methods, which has big implications for cryptography and internet security. Another famous one, Grover’s algorithm, can search through large datasets much more quickly than classical algorithms.



Current Problems and Challenges:

Right now, quantum computers are probably sounding like a fantasy super machine. However, they are still in very early stages of development and have many problems.

One of these current problems with it is that Qubits are very sensitive to their environment, to where even the slightest vibrations, temperature change, or even any background radiation can completely mess it up.

That’s why they need to be kept at extremely low temperatures (such as sub-zero). To fix this, researchers are working on something called quantum error correction, which will help stabilize qubits, making calculations more reliable.


Another challenge is Scalability. Right now(not including Microsoft's Majorana 1 Chip, which you will learn about later), most quantum computers only have tens or hundreds of qubits, but experts say that we may need millions for them to reach their full potential.

This makes building larger quantum systems one of the biggest current engineering challenges. Along with this, price is also a big factor in scalability. The reason behind this is that, currently, quantum computers are extremely expensive and only available to big companies, research labs, and universities.



Key Aspects and Definitions
  • Qubits: A qubit is similar to a regular bit(which can either be a 1 or a 0); however, a qubit can be both a 1 and 0, thus allowing qubits to hold more information and perform computations in a more parallel way

  • Superposition: A qubit can exist in multiple states (0 and 1) simultaneously until measured, enabling quantum computers to explore many possibilities concurrently.

  • Entanglement: The states of multiple qubits can be linked, meaning they are correlated even if physically separated. This correlated behavior can be exploited to perform computations more efficiently.

  • Quantum algorithms: Quantum computers use specialized algorithms, like Shor's algorithm for factoring large numbers, that can potentially solve problems faster than classical algorithms.

  • Scalability: The scalability of quantum computers will be one of the challenges that we will face.

  • Quantum error correction: A set of techniques and algorithms made to identify and fix errors in quantum computers while also protecting the information stored in qubits from errors and decoherence.

  • Decoherence: The process by which a quantum system loses its unique quantum properties, such as superposition and entanglement, by interacting with its environment



Majorana 1 chip
What is the Majorana 1 chip?

The Majorana 1 is the world's first quantum processor based on topological qubits, a fundamentally new kind of qubit protected by the principles of topology rather than the more frail quantum states.

It’s built using a topoconductor, which is a hybrid material made from indium arsenide (semiconductor) and aluminum (superconductor) that achieves a new topological superconducting state.


Why this new state of matter matters:

If you're reading this, chances are that you know of the three(four if you include plasma) states of matter: liquid, solid, and gas. However, Microsoft has created a whole new state of matter.

This topological state of matter doesn't behave like ordinary solids, liquids, or gases. It enables Majorana zero modes(or MZM), exotic quasiparticles theorized decades ago, that can encode quantum information in a way highly resistant to noise, making it much less frail.

In this setup, the information isn’t stored in a fragile superposition but rather in the presence or absence of an extra electron shared between two Majorana zero modes, making the data almost immune to disturbances.


How It Works: Chips, Pulses, and Measurements:
  • Qubit Structure: Each qubit is formed by configuring aluminum nanowires into an “H” shape, with four MZMs controlling one qubit. These H-shaped units can be tiled across the chip for scalability.

  • Measurement-Based Control: Rather than relying on precisely shaped analog pulses like many quantum systems, Majorana-1 uses digital control. You merely flip a switch to connect a quantum dot to the nanowire and send simple measurement pulses, meaning timing and shape don’t need to be perfect.

  • Reading the Qubit: When the quantum dot connects to the nanowire, the change in its electrical charge depends on the qubit’s parity. This change alters how microwaves reflect off the dot, letting us infer the qubit’s state.

Summary

Quantum computing works by utilizing qubits, which can exist in multiple states simultaneously and be linked through entanglement, unlike classical bits that are strictly 0 or 1. These properties make it so that quantum computers can process more possibilities in parallel and solve certain problems, such as factoring large numbers (Shor's algorithm) and searching datasets (Grover's algorithm), much faster than classical methods.

However, despite their potential, quantum computers are in early development, facing challenges like environmental sensitivity requiring extreme cooling, the need for quantum error correction, and scalability issues to reach millions of qubits for full potential. A demonstration of quantum supremacy was achieved by Google in 2019, solving a task much faster than classical supercomputers, though they are not yet suitable for everyday use.

While currently expensive and accessible primarily to institutions, cloud platforms are increasing remote access for wider experimentation, paving the way for future revolutions in medicine, materials science, AI, and climate modeling.


Summary for Majorana 1

In a nutshell, Majorana 1 by Microsoft introduces a radical new route to quantum computing. By harnessing topological quantum states and adopting digital measurement-based control, it promises qubits that are more stable, scalable, and simpler to manage.

Now I know you're probably thinking, "wow, this is so cool! There's no way there are any downsides to this!" And you'd be wrong(besides the first part). While the theoretical foundation is compelling, a real-world demonstration of large-scale, error-corrected operation is still future work.
